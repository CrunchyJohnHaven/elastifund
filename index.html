Residual time value mispricing may exist but is corrected too quickly by market makers. The signal is too sparse for systematic trading.

Education Center

Lessons from the Lab

Organized by difficulty. Each has a 30-second TL;DR and a technical deep dive. Derived from real testing, not theory.

Beginner

What Are Prediction Markets?

Markets where you bet on whether real-world events will happen. Prices reflect the crowd's probability estimate. If you're more accurate than the crowd, you profit.
Unlike sports betting, prediction markets have no house edge on maker orders. The market itself is the counterparty. This changes the entire cost structure of systematic trading.
Beginner

Anti-Anchoring: Why the AI Can't See the Price

If the AI sees the current market price before estimating probability, it unconsciously anchors to that price. We blind the AI to prevent the most common forecasting bias.
Anchoring bias is well-documented in psychology and quantitative finance. Our backtests confirmed it: AI estimates made with price visibility had 8-12% higher correlation with current prices than blind estimates. The blind estimate is worse at predicting the current price but better at predicting the outcome.
Intermediate

The Maker/Taker Fee Asymmetry

On Polymarket, takers pay up to 1.56% per trade. Makers pay 0%. This single fact killed 3 of our 10 initial strategies and revived one we'd rejected.
The fee formula is polynomial: Fee = C × p × 0.25 × (p(1-p))^2. Maximum at p=0.50. Analysis of 72.1M trades shows makers earn +1.12% excess vs takers at -1.12%. A 2.24% round-trip difference. Every strategy must be evaluated under maker-only assumptions.
Intermediate

Kelly Criterion: Why We Use Quarter-Kelly

Full Kelly maximizes long-term growth but with 36.9% ruin probability when your edge estimates are uncertain. Quarter-Kelly sacrifices some growth for 0% ruin and 8% max drawdown.
Kelly is optimal only when you know your exact edge. We don't. Our edge estimates have uncertainty, and full Kelly amplifies that uncertainty into catastrophic drawdowns. Quarter-Kelly is the standard choice in professional quant trading for exactly this reason.
Intermediate

Kill Rules: Pre-Registered Hypothesis Testing

Define your rejection criteria BEFORE testing. This prevents data mining and self-deception. Our 6 kill rules are published before every experiment runs.
Kill rules: minimum signal count, positive OOS expected value, win rate above break-even, cost stress survival, temporal stability, and maximum drawdown limit. Walk-forward validation with 70/30 temporal split ensures no lookahead bias. If a strategy fails any kill rule, it's rejected regardless of how promising the in-sample results look.
Advanced

Favorite-Longshot Bias in Prediction Markets

The market systematically overprices longshots (YES at low probability) and underprices favorites (NO at high probability). We exploit this with asymmetric thresholds: 5% for NO, 15% for YES.
This bias is well-documented in horse racing, sports betting, and now prediction markets. The mechanism: retail participants are drawn to cheap YES contracts (low price, high potential payout). This inflates YES prices and depresses NO prices. Our asymmetric thresholds improved win rate from 64.9% to 76.2% on NO-side bets.
Advanced

LMSR Bayesian Pricing

Logarithmic Market Scoring Rule provides a theoretically grounded way to update probability estimates as new evidence arrives. We use it as a signal source alongside the LLM ensemble.
LMSR, invented by Robin Hanson, is the pricing mechanism behind many prediction markets. We reverse-engineer it as a signal: by tracking how the market's LMSR-implied probability changes in response to order flow, we can distinguish informed trading from noise. 828ms target cycle time for real-time Bayesian updating in log-space.
Advanced

Why Most Quant Strategies Fail on Prediction Markets

We tested 10 families of traditional quant strategies. 9 failed. Statistical patterns, speed-based approaches, and brute-force ML all break down because prediction markets aren't financial markets.
Prediction markets have fundamentally different microstructure: no continuous price discovery, binary outcomes, event-driven resolution, and crypto-native 24/7 trading. Strategies designed for equity markets (mean reversion, momentum, volatility trading) assume properties that don't exist here. What works: domain-specific reasoning, probabilistic calibration, and structural edges in fee mechanics.
