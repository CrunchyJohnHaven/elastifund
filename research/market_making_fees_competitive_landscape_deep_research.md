# Market Making Strategy on Polymarket (Prompt 1)  

**Bottom Line:** Market-making on Polymarket means continuously quoting both sides of a binary market via its CLOB API. One must split capital into YES/NO tokens and place simultaneous buy and sell orders, then dynamically rebalance to manage inventory and capture the bid-ask spread【33†L139-L147】【47†L359-L366】. Success requires tight risk controls, since uninformed traders (and faster bots) can pick off stale quotes. Maker orders pay no fee, so the edge comes from spreads, liquidity subsidies, and arbitrage. Realistic returns are modest with small capital; risks include adverse selection, inventory imbalances, and platform outages.  

1. **CLOB mechanics & quoting:** Polymarket’s exchange is a central limit order book on Polygon. A market maker first converts USDC.e into equal YES and NO outcome tokens (a *split* operation) to hold both sides【33†L139-L147】. The bot then uses the Polymarket API (REST or SDK) to place limit buy orders on one side and sell orders on the other (often using GTC/GTD orders). The docs stress **“quote both sides”** of the market and refresh quotes rapidly【47†L359-L366】. Practically, one issues bid and ask orders at symmetric prices around a fair value. For efficiency, makers use batch order submissions and WebSocket feeds to update quotes in real time【47†L370-L378】. Key best practices include skewing quotes based on inventory, cancelling stale orders on market moves, and using price limits to avoid “negative spreads” (which cause guaranteed loss)【47†L359-L366】【1†L193-L200】.  

2. **Inventory management:** Because Polymarket outcomes are binary, you must balance your YES/NO token holdings. A common strategy is: if inventory becomes **imbalanced** (too many YES tokens), skew your quotes to favor selling YES or switch capital to NO. Polymarket docs recommend *“skew quotes when inventory becomes imbalanced,”* merging excess tokens back into USDC.e, and splitting more USDC.e when inventory on a side runs low【35†L337-L344】. In practice, bots adjust their bid/ask midpoints or quantities to lean against their skew. For example, if you hold more YES than NO, raise your YES sell price (or lower your YES buy price) to encourage sellers. Also set hard position limits. If one side gets too large, you can **merge** YES+NO pairs back to USDC.e to neutralize exposure【35†L337-L344】. These dynamic rebalancing steps avoid being *“stuck holding one side”* when the market moves.  

3. **Fee economics:** Polymarket makes market makers **fee-free**. Most markets (all non-crypto, non-sports) charge 0% to both makers and takers【47†L339-L347】. Taker (market order) fees exist only on new crypto markets (from Mar 6, 2026) and certain sports markets (NCAA basketball, Serie A from Feb 18, 2026)【47†L339-L347】【10†L158-L166】. The taker fee formula is:  
   $$\text{fee} = C \times p \times \text{feeRate} \times (p(1-p))^{\text{exponent}},$$  
   where \(p\) is price. For sports, feeRate = 0.0175, exponent = 1; for crypto, feeRate = 0.25, exponent = 2【4†L176-L184】. Thus at \(p=0.5\), crypto takers pay \(\sim1.56\%\) of trade value and sports takers pay \(\sim0.44\%\)【6†L222-L230】. Importantly **makers pay zero** and actually receive a rebate (20% of fees on crypto, 25% on sports)【4†L176-L184】. In effect, a maker earns the full spread plus any rebate. For a taker, these fees impose a drag: for example at \(p=0.50\) a crypto buyer loses ~1.56% in fees, so must have an edge of about 0.78% to break even. Using the fee table【6†L222-L230】, the *breakeven edge* (half the effective fee) is roughly: 0.10→0.03%, 0.30→0.40%, 0.50→0.78%, 0.70→0.40%, 0.90→0.03% (crypto markets; sports fees are ~55% smaller).  

4. **Existing market-making bots:** Several open-source projects implement MM strategies. For example, **lorine93s’ Polymarket MM bot** actively quotes both sides with optimal spreads and rebalances inventory【17†L653-L657】. **Warproxxx’s “Poly-Maker”** is a comprehensive Python bot: it monitors the live orderbook via WebSockets, maintains two-sided orders with configurable parameters, merges positions, and applies sophisticated spread and risk management【20†L304-L312】. A simpler example is **gigi0500’s bot**, which defaults to 0.50% spreads and dynamically adjusts quotes based on position【19†L7-L14】. In general, these bots do *(a)* continuous two-sided quoting, *(b)* spread management (often static or algorithmic), and *(c)* inventory skewing. (They do **not** bet directionally; their edge is the spread). Other frameworks exist: Polymarket Agents provides an LLM-powered trading framework【25†L289-L293】, and the “discountry” bot is a trading framework implementing a flash-crash arbitrage strategy【24†L343-L352】.  

5. **Pro vs. retail approach:** Institutional market-makers (e.g. Susquehanna) treat prediction markets like any other quant venue. For instance, Susquehanna is openly recruiting prediction-market traders to “build real-time models” for event outcomes【70†L202-L210】. Such pros emphasize statistical rigor, low-latency execution, and multi-market hedging. They likely implement advanced MM models (e.g. stochastic optimal spread with event risk adjustments) and portfolio-level hedges across related markets. Retail bots, by contrast, often rely on simpler heuristics or AI signals. Anecdotes suggest most profitable bots exploit **arbitrage and speed**, not fundamental insight【52†L127-L134】. A successful retail trader put it: the real edge is “knowing new information before everyone else” and reacting in milliseconds【52†L127-L134】. In short, professionals bring deeper quant research and tech (real-time data, risk limits, large capital), whereas retail bots often use off-the-shelf strategies and smaller bets.  

6. **Expected P&L:** With $1–10K capital, market making is modestly profitable at best. Published guides estimate roughly \$50–200/month on \$1–5K, and \$200–\$1K/month on \$5–25K【44†L401-L408】. Scaling up, \$25–100K might yield \$1–5K/month, and \$100K+ yields \$5–20K/month【44†L401-L408】. (These assume average market conditions and active volume.) Actual results vary: top bots have made six to seven figures (e.g. one account shows $16.62M cumulative profit【53†L28-L31】), but they also risked millions. Liquidity-reward subsidies (Polymarket’s bonus program) can add 5–15% returns for active two-sided quoting. However, small makers should temper expectations: many traders report only single-digit or low double-digit percent APR from market making. The bid-ask spread is often only a few cents on a dollar, so profits grow slowly relative to capital.  

7. **Risks:** Market making faces **adverse selection**: if news hits, your stale quotes get picked off. For example, a guide warns: quoting 0.50/0.52 is dangerous when a sudden jump to 0.90 occurs – you could buy at 0.52 and immediately be holding a near-worthless contract【44†L333-L341】. *Inventory risk* is also serious: “holding too much inventory on the losing side means total loss of that position”【44†L244-L252】. Thus one must continuously rebalance or cap positions. **Platform risk** includes blockchain outages or bugs: Polymarket once went down during a Polygon outage (Dec 2025)【46†L133-L140】. During such events your assets or positions may be frozen. Also, regulatory or policy changes (e.g. fee rollouts) can alter profitability. Finally, **crowding risk** (“alpha decay”): since Polymarket now has many sophisticated bots, easy opportunities vanish quickly. Polymarket even added fees and latency limits to curb arbitrage bots【51†L195-L200】, indicating diminishing edges. In summary, market-making can work, but only with rigorous risk controls and constant adaptation.  

# Polymarket Fee Structure Deep Dive (Prompt 2)  

**Bottom Line:** Polymarket’s fee model is mostly zero for makers and takers, with a few exceptions. Only new **crypto** markets (from Mar 6, 2026) and certain **sports** markets (NCAA basketball, Serie A, from Feb 18, 2026) charge taker fees【47†L339-L347】【10†L158-L166】. All other markets remain fee-free【47†L339-L347】. The taker-fee formula is fee = C·p·feeRate·(p(1–p))^exponent【4†L176-L184】, with parameters: Sports (feeRate=0.0175, exp=1, max≈0.44%) and Crypto (feeRate=0.25, exp=2, max≈1.56%)【4†L176-L184】【6†L222-L230】. Maker orders **always pay 0%** (and even receive rebates from these fees)【47†L339-L347】【4†L152-L160】. There are no Polymarket withdrawal or trading fees in other categories【15†L193-L196】. Gas/network costs apply separately. Compared to peers, Polymarket is low-cost: e.g. Kalshi charges a fee on expected winnings【41†L328-L336】, whereas Polymarket is free on most markets. No further fee changes have been announced for Q2 2026 beyond those rollouts【10†L138-L147】【10†L158-L166】. The break-even “edge” vs. fees (approx. half the taker fee) is tiny at extremes (≈0.03%) and about 0.4–0.8% near p=0.30–0.70【6†L222-L230】.  

1. **Markets with fees:** Polymarket’s docs list all fee-enabled markets: *“All crypto markets (starting March 6, 2026)”* and *“Select sports (e.g. NCAAB, Serie A, starting Feb 18, 2026)”*【47†L339-L347】【10†L158-L166】. No other markets have taker fees – e.g. financial or election markets remain zero-fee【47†L339-L347】. This is the complete list as of Mar 2026. In short, if the market is crypto or one of the specified sports, takers pay a fee; makers (limit orders) pay none.  

2. **Fee formula:** The taker fee is a price-dependent function. Per trade (size C):  
   $$\text{fee} = C\times p \times \text{feeRate} \times (p(1-p))^{\text{exponent}}.$$
   The constants are: for sports (NCAA/SerieA), feeRate = 0.0175, exponent = 1 (so fee = C·0.0175·p·(p(1-p))). For crypto markets, feeRate = 0.25, exponent = 2【4†L176-L184】. Thus sports taker fee = 0.0175·p·p(1-p); crypto taker fee = 0.25·p·(p(1-p))^2. The peak effective fee (fee divided by trade value) is about 0.44% at p=0.5 for sports, and 1.56% at p=0.5 for crypto【6†L222-L230】. (At the extreme prices 0.1 or 0.9, the fee is only ≈0.06% of trade value【6†L222-L230】.)  

3. **Maker fee:** Polymarket explicitly charges *no* fee to limit orders【47†L339-L347】. Instead, any taker fees collected are redistributed as maker rebates (20% for crypto, 25% for sports)【4†L176-L184】. In effect, if you place a passive bid or ask that gets filled, you pay $0 fee and actually earn a small rebate. There are no hidden maker fees on any market.  

4. **Other costs:** Polymarket itself does not charge deposit or withdrawal fees【15†L193-L196】. You can withdraw USDC.e immediately and freely via the bridge. However, standard blockchain costs still apply: gas fees on Polygon for settling trades or splits. (Polymarket offers a gasless option through its relayer for trades if you use their API key【12†L153-L161】, but otherwise you must pay.) Intermediary services (like Coinbase for on/off ramps) may add fees. There are no additional Polymarket “platform fees” beyond what the docs state.  

5. **Comparison:** Relative to other platforms, Polymarket is very competitive. For example, Kalshi (regulated CFTC market) charges a fee on expected profit (their maker/taker fee is typically ~0.03 of the odds shift)【41†L328-L336】. By contrast, Polymarket’s core markets are fee-free. Even on the fee-bearing markets, Polymarket’s maximum 1.56% taker fee is comparable to or lower than many sportsbook vig rates. Metaculus or man-made “play money” markets charge nothing since they use points, so Polymarket’s fee model (zero to modest) is in line with crypto prediction norms.  

6. **Upcoming changes:** The only known changes in H1 2026 were the sports and crypto expansions above【10†L138-L147】【10†L158-L166】. We found *no* official announcements of new fees or rate hikes beyond those. Polymarket’s changelog confirms the timetable for crypto (March 6) and sports (Feb 18)【10†L138-L147】【10†L158-L166】. Traders should watch Polymarket’s official channels for any Q2 updates, but as of March 2026 the fee structure is stable.  

7. **Breakeven edge:** To overcome taker fees, your “edge” must exceed the fee cost. Using the effective fee rates from the crypto schedule【6†L222-L230】, the approximate edge needed (in percentage of price) is:  
   - At $p=0.10$: fee ≈0.06% ⇒ edge ≈0.03% (negligible)  
   - At $p=0.30$: fee ≈0.80% ⇒ edge ≈0.40%  
   - At $p=0.50$: fee ≈1.56% ⇒ edge ≈0.78%  
   - At $p=0.70$: fee ≈0.80% ⇒ edge ≈0.40%  
   - At $p=0.90$: fee ≈0.06% ⇒ edge ≈0.03%  
   (In sports markets, use ~40% of these values since their peak fee is only 0.44%【6†L222-L230】.)  These are the minimum probability advantages a taker must have to break even net of fees. Makers (limit orders) do not pay fees, so they only need to beat the spread.  

# Competitive Landscape on Polymarket (Prompt 3)  

**Bottom Line:** Polymarket is now dominated by automated traders. HFT-style bots and quant funds (e.g. the OpenClaw framework) have turned modest capital into seven-figure profits【51†L189-L197】【53†L28-L31】. Common strategies include arbitrage (cross-market or temporal), tight market-making, and LLM/sentiment analysis, but success has concentrated in a few well-capitalized bots. Retail returns have largely evaporated as bots copied winning plays.  Open-source frameworks exist (Polymarket Agents, market-maker bots) but competitive edges are thin.  

1. **Top bots/funds:** Public data and reports identify a few big players. The “OpenClaw” bot suite is notorious: one account (0x8dxd) executed ~20,000 trades and earned ~$1.7M profit in a recent period【51†L189-L197】. Another big winner is a user “Fredi9999” whose Polymarket profile shows an *all-time* P&L of \$16.62M【53†L28-L31】. (These are pooled profits from many markets.) Smaller examples: LinkedIn reports one trader drained ~$233K from bot accounts in two nights by outmaneuvering them【61†L39-L47】. Other public handles like “Aleksandmoney” have appeared in anecdotes. In sum, the top ~10 automated traders have capital on the order of millions of USD, deploying large stake sizes.  

2. **Strategies used:** Profitable bots overwhelmingly exploit market inefficiencies. The most common tactics are:  
   - **Arbitrage:** Exploiting price gaps between related markets (e.g. Polymarket vs Kalshi) or temporal spreads. Community discussions indicate successful traders focus on arbitrage instead of pure directional bets【52†L127-L134】【52†L163-L173】.  
   - **Market-making:** Posting narrow bid/ask pairs around consensus. For instance, the $115K/week OpenClaw bot reportedly provided liquidity on multiple markets, capturing the spread【52†L168-L173】.  
   - **Sentiment/LLM forecasts:** Some bots incorporate news or LLM models to predict outcomes or regime changes【52†L175-L178】, especially for fast-moving crypto questions. However, even these use strict risk rules and often just trade around model outputs.  
   In practice, bots use high-speed data ingestion (WebSockets, oracles, news feeds) and algorithmic logic. They rely on having the fastest execution – one trader notes, the *biggest edge is knowing news before others* and acting in milliseconds【52†L127-L134】.  

3. **Reported returns:** Exact figures are scarce, but some snapshots are striking. The FlyPix blog noted the OpenClaw system made \$115K in one week【52†L71-L79】. The Phemex report cited above found the 0x8dxd bot earned ~$1.7M over 20k trades【51†L189-L197】. Polymarket’s own liquidity incentives paid out over \$20M to market makers in 2024【27†L409-L413】. Individual makers have reported \$200–\$800 per day in peak periods【27†L409-L413】. By contrast, the average retail player often loses; one analysis remarked that only *“a minority of traders achieve consistent profitability,”* and that *“the bots that do succeed typically employ sophisticated arbitrage strategies rather than directional betting”*【52†L127-L134】.  

4. **Capital deployed:** Bots range from modest to massive size. Many start with tens of thousands of USD, but the biggest deploy millions. For example, in a reported event, a trader used a \$1M XRP trade (with \$10k margin) to swing a Polymarket bet【61†L39-L47】 – a scale far beyond typical retail. Polymarket’s leaderboards hint at multi-million-dollar positions (e.g. Fredi9999 shows ~$9.7M in active positions). Overall, we estimate that at least **tens of millions** of USD is under automated trading on Polymarket, given the volume and observed P&L of top accounts.  

5. **Open-source bot frameworks:** Several actively maintained projects exist. *Polymarket Agents* is an official SDK for building AI trading bots【25†L289-L293】. The “polymarket-market-maker-bot” repos (e.g. by lorine93s and warproxxx) provide templates for two-sided quoting【17†L653-L657】【20†L304-L312】. The *discountry* Polymarket-trading-bot handles flash-crash arbitrage scenarios【24†L343-L352】. There are also simpler examples (like the gigi0500 bot in [19]) and numerous analysis tools (Polyprophet, PolyRadar, etc.). Importantly, many of these projects are forks or inspiration for each other; the community is small enough that ideas spread quickly.  

6. **Academic/industry analysis:** Recent studies confirm the bot dominance. For instance, an academic paper (Apr 2024–Apr 2025 data) finds that arbitrage trading is prevalent on Polymarket【67†L269-L278】. Crypto media report that automated strategies have turned “small stakes into jaw-dropping profits” in ultra-short markets【56†L25-L33】. There are also anecdotes (e.g. CFTC warnings) about fraudsters and bots targeting Polymarket. In short, the literature (and press) consistently notes that most automated trading is for spreads/arbitrage, and that retail “signals” often underperform simple quant strategies.  

7. **Alpha decay:** With so many bots competing, edges erode quickly. Polymarket has already reacted by adding fees and random delays to slow arbitrary (see Q2)【51†L195-L200】. Community chatter suggests that strategies like plain arbitrage or naive sentiment following are now well-known and yield diminishing returns. As one insider said, markets aren’t just about being “right” on price – timing and structure matter【61†L39-L47】. Any simple pattern is soon copied, so surviving alpha likely requires novel data or models. In practice, this means constant adaptation: for example, some bots now watch for predictable weekend-liquidity anomalies【61†L118-L121】 or use machine learning ensembles. The key takeaway: profitable strategies tend to **decay** as more bots replicate them, so one should be cautious about “low-hanging fruit.”
